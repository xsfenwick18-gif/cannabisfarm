Autoflowers vs Photoperiods

This is one of the first real decisions every grower makes, and it shapes your entire approach from seed to harvest.

The core difference

Photoperiod plants flower in response to a change in their daily light cycle — specifically, receiving roughly 12 hours of uninterrupted darkness signals the plant to begin flowering. This means a grower has full control over when flowering starts simply by adjusting the light schedule, and it also means photoperiod plants can be kept in vegetative growth indefinitely under longer light schedules.

Autoflowering plants, by contrast, begin flowering automatically based on age — typically around day 30-40 from germination — regardless of the light schedule they're under. This trait comes from cannabis ruderalis genetics, a subspecies adapted to short growing seasons at northern latitudes, bred into modern autoflowering hybrids.

Practical differences for growers

  • Total grow time: Autoflowers typically finish in 10-12 weeks from seed to harvest. Photoperiods take longer overall — often 4-16+ weeks of vegetative growth (grower's choice) plus 7-11 weeks of flowering.
  • Size control: Photoperiods can be kept small or grown large depending on how long you veg them. Autoflowers have a fixed, shorter growth window and generally stay more compact regardless of conditions.
  • Stress tolerance: Autoflowers have less time to recover from stress (transplant shock, heavy training, nutrient problems) since their life cycle is fixed and short. Photoperiods generally tolerate more stress and recovery time since vegetative growth can simply be extended.
  • Scheduling flexibility: Photoperiods require accurate, uninterrupted light scheduling once flowering begins. Autoflowers don't need a schedule change at all, which simplifies setups where consistent light control is harder to achieve.

Which to choose

For a true first grow, autoflowers are generally the more forgiving and faster option — fewer scheduling requirements, shorter total time investment, and a simpler overall process. Photoperiods reward more experience and offer more control once you understand timing, training, and light scheduling, making them a strong choice once you've completed at least one successful autoflower grow.

Critical autoflower rule: never transplant an autoflower. Plant directly into its final pot from day one — the root disturbance from transplanting can cost an autoflower days or weeks of growth it simply doesn't have time to recover, given its fixed life cycle.
