Soil & Growing Media

Your grow medium is the foundation everything else depends on. Get this right and many other problems simply don't happen.

Choosing a growing medium

Three mediums dominate home cannabis growing:

  • Living soil: A biologically active mix containing organic matter, beneficial microbes, and slow-release nutrients like worm castings. Requires less precise feeding because the soil ecosystem buffers and regulates nutrient availability.
  • Coco coir: An inert, fast-draining medium made from coconut husk fiber. Offers more control over feeding since you supply all nutrients directly, but requires more frequent watering and precise pH management.
  • Soilless mixes: Peat or coco-based blends with added perlite, sitting between the two above in terms of control and forgiveness.

Why perlite matters

Perlite is a lightweight, porous volcanic material mixed into soil to improve drainage and aeration. Without it, soil compacts over time and the air pockets roots need to breathe disappear, leading to waterlogged, oxygen-starved roots — a major cause of stunted growth and root rot. As a general guideline, mix in roughly 20-30% perlite by volume for indoor pots, increasing toward 30-40% for outdoor setups in wetter climates where drainage matters even more.

Pre-soaking your soil

Dry soil straight from the bag repels water unevenly at first, creating dry pockets even after watering. Always moisten your soil mix thoroughly before planting — it should feel like a wrung-out sponge, damp throughout but not dripping. This ensures your seed or seedling has consistent access to moisture from day one.

Understanding pH

Soil pH controls how available each nutrient is to the plant's roots, regardless of how much of that nutrient is actually present in the soil. Cannabis grown in soil generally prefers a pH range of 6.0-7.0; in coco or hydro setups, the ideal range shifts slightly lower, around 5.5-6.5. Outside these ranges, certain nutrients become "locked out" even if they're abundant in the medium, which is why pH problems often look identical to nutrient deficiencies.

Drainage tip: raise pots slightly off the ground (bricks work well) rather than using a sitting saucer underneath. This allows excess water to drain away completely rather than pooling at the bottom of the pot, which helps prevent root rot.
