Topic 09

Pests & Diseases

Catching a pest or disease problem early is the difference between a minor inconvenience and losing an entire grow.

Spider mites

Tiny, barely visible pests that feed on leaf undersides, causing small white or yellow speckling on leaves and, in advanced infestations, fine webbing. They thrive in hot, dry conditions, so maintaining adequate humidity helps prevent outbreaks. Early detection (checking leaf undersides regularly with a magnifying glass) is key, since populations multiply extremely quickly once established.

Fungus gnats

Small flying insects whose larvae live in moist soil, feeding on organic matter and sometimes young roots. They're generally more of an annoyance than a serious threat in small numbers, but large populations can damage root systems, especially in seedlings. Letting the top layer of soil dry out between waterings significantly reduces fungus gnat populations, since their larvae need consistently moist soil to thrive. Yellow sticky traps placed near the soil surface are an effective, low-effort way to monitor and reduce adult populations.

Powdery mildew

A fungal disease appearing as white, powdery patches on leaves, typically starting on lower or interior growth where airflow is weakest. It thrives in high humidity with poor air circulation. Prevention is far easier than treatment: ensure good airflow throughout the canopy (a small oscillating fan helps), avoid overcrowding plants, and keep humidity within appropriate ranges for the growth stage. If caught early, removing affected leaves and improving airflow can stop it from spreading; advanced cases are much harder to fully eliminate.

Root rot

Caused by overwatering and poor drainage, root rot involves harmful pathogens colonising waterlogged, oxygen-starved root systems. It often shows above ground as wilting despite wet soil, and a sour or rotten smell from the growing medium is a telltale sign. Prevention comes down to proper drainage (adequate perlite, pots raised off the ground) and only watering when the soil is actually dry enough to need it.

General prevention principle: most pest and disease problems are environment-driven rather than random bad luck. Stable temperature, appropriate humidity for the growth stage, good airflow, and not overwatering prevent the large majority of issues before they start.
