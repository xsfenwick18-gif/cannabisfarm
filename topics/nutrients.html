Topic 04

Nutrients & Feeding

Feeding is where overconfidence causes the most damage. Understanding the basics prevents most feeding mistakes before they start.

Understanding NPK

NPK refers to the three primary macronutrients plants need: Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Potassium (K). Cannabis needs different ratios at different stages — higher nitrogen during vegetative growth to build leaves and stems, and higher phosphorus and potassium during flowering to support bud development. Most commercial nutrient lines offer separate "grow" and "bloom" formulas reflecting this shift.

Deficiency vs toxicity

It's easy to mistake one for the other, since both can cause yellowing or discoloured leaves. As a general rule: deficiencies tend to show first on older, lower leaves (since the plant pulls nutrients from older growth to support new growth), while nutrient toxicity or nutrient burn typically shows on the leaf tips first, often as a "burnt" brown edge, and can appear on newer growth as well.

When in doubt, the safer move is almost always to underfeed rather than overfeed. A nutrient deficiency is usually correctable within days of adjusting feed; nutrient toxicity can take much longer to flush out and recover from.

Organic vs synthetic

Organic feeding (like worm castings, compost, and organic liquid feeds) works through soil biology breaking down nutrients slowly over time, which is more forgiving of feeding mistakes since there's a natural buffer. Synthetic nutrients deliver nutrients directly and immediately in mineral salt form, offering more precise control but less margin for error if you overfeed.

For beginners, an organic approach — particularly using quality worm castings mixed into your soil from day one — significantly reduces the complexity of feeding, since the soil itself does much of the regulating work.

Worm castings as a base

Worm castings are a slow-release, microbially rich organic fertiliser that can supply sufficient nutrition for an entire grow cycle — vegetative through flowering — when mixed into soil at planting. This approach avoids the need for a complex feeding schedule and reduces the risk of nutrient burn, since the castings release nutrients gradually rather than all at once.

Watering rule: never water on a fixed schedule. Check the soil by feel first — if the top 2-3cm feels dry, it's time to water. Overwatering is just as harmful as underwatering and is one of the most common mistakes new growers make.
