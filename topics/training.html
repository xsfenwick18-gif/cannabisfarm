Training Techniques

Training reshapes how your plant grows, exposing more bud sites to light and improving both yield and bud quality.

Why train at all

Left untrained, cannabis grows in a natural Christmas-tree shape — one dominant top cola and progressively smaller buds further down, where light barely reaches. Training techniques redistribute the plant's growth so that more bud sites receive strong, direct light, which generally means more total usable bud at harvest, and more even bud quality across the plant rather than one large top cola and many small popcorn buds beneath it.

Low Stress Training (LST)

LST involves gently bending and tying down the main stem and branches (using soft plant ties or twine) to create a flatter, wider canopy. This is the gentlest training method since it doesn't involve cutting the plant, making it a good entry point for beginners. It's typically done during the vegetative stage, tying branches down a little at a time as the plant grows.

Topping

Topping means cutting off the very top growth tip of the main stem, which removes apical dominance and causes the plant to develop two main colas instead of one from that point onward. This creates a bushier, more even plant. Topping is best done early in the vegetative stage, giving the plant plenty of time to recover and redirect growth before flowering begins.

SCROG (Screen of Green)

SCROG involves placing a horizontal screen or net above the plants and weaving growing branches through it as they reach upward. This forces an extremely flat, even canopy and is particularly effective for maximising yield in a limited growing area, since it makes full use of available light across the entire screen rather than concentrating growth in one tall cola.

Timing matters: most training techniques should be done during the vegetative stage and stopped once flowering begins, since the plant needs to focus its energy on bud development rather than recovering from new stress. Light LST can sometimes continue early in flower, but topping and more invasive techniques should not.

Autoflowers and training

Because autoflowers have a fixed, shorter life cycle, they have less time to recover from stress. Gentle LST is generally fine, but more aggressive techniques like topping carry more risk of setting back an autoflower's limited growth window — many experienced growers avoid heavy training on autos for this reason.
