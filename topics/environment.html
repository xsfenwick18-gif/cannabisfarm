Topic 10

Climate & Environment

Your environment shapes everything — get temperature, humidity, and airflow right, and many other problems simply don't arise.

Temperature targets

Cannabis generally grows best within 20-28°C during the light period, with a slight drop (a few degrees) acceptable and even beneficial during the dark period. Temperatures consistently above 30°C can cause heat stress, visible as upward-curling, taco-shaped leaves; temperatures below 15-18°C can slow growth and increase susceptibility to mould and disease.

Humidity by growth stage

Humidity needs change significantly across the plant's life cycle:

  • Seedling stage: High humidity, around 65-75%, helps young plants that don't yet have fully developed root systems to absorb adequate moisture.
  • Vegetative stage: Moderate humidity, around 50-65%, supports vigorous leaf growth.
  • Flowering stage: Lower humidity, around 40-50%, reduces the risk of mould and bud rot forming inside dense, developing buds.

Understanding VPD

VPD (Vapour Pressure Deficit) combines temperature and humidity into a single measurement that more accurately reflects how easily a plant can transpire (move moisture and nutrients through its system) than either factor measured alone. Two grow spaces with identical humidity readings can have very different VPD if their temperatures differ, leading to different levels of plant stress despite the humidity looking "correct" on paper.

While not essential for beginners to actively track, understanding that temperature and humidity work together — not independently — helps explain why following humidity targets alone sometimes doesn't fully solve plant stress issues.

Why airflow matters

Good airflow does more than just regulate temperature. It strengthens stems through gentle constant movement, helps prevent mould and mildew by not allowing humid air to stagnate around leaves and buds, and supports healthy gas exchange (plants need fresh CO2 reaching their leaves, and stale air depletes locally available CO2 faster than it's replenished). A small oscillating fan moving air gently across the canopy — without blasting directly onto plants — is generally sufficient for most home setups.

South African context: humid coastal regions like the Western Cape in wet seasons may need extra attention to airflow and dehumidification during flowering to prevent bud rot, while drier inland regions may need to monitor for the opposite problem — humidity dropping too low, which can stress plants and slow growth.
