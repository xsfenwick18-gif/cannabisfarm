Lighting Explained

Light is the single biggest input you control. Understanding what your plant actually needs from it prevents most early mistakes.

LED vs HPS

LED grow lights have largely replaced HPS (High Pressure Sodium) lights for home growing, and for good reason: they run cooler, use less electricity for the same light output, and modern full-spectrum LEDs produce excellent results across all growth stages. HPS still has loyal users because of its proven track record and lower upfront cost, but the running costs and heat output make LED the more practical choice for most indoor setups today.

Understanding DLI

DLI (Daily Light Integral) measures the total amount of usable light your plant receives over a full 24-hour period, rather than just the intensity at any one moment. This matters because two different combinations of light duration and intensity can deliver the same total DLI, but a plant given 18 hours of moderate light may respond differently than one given 12 hours of intense light. As a general guide: seedlings do well around 100-200 DLI equivalent, vegetative plants around 400-600, and flowering plants benefit from the highest range, often 600-1000+, depending on the strain and setup.

Light schedules

For photoperiod plants, the standard schedules are:

  • Seedling/Veg: 18 hours light / 6 hours dark, sometimes 20/4 for faster vegetative growth.
  • Flowering: 12 hours light / 12 hours uninterrupted dark. This light change is what triggers flowering in photoperiod strains — any light leak during the dark period can stress the plant or cause it to revert to vegetative growth.

Autoflowering strains don't need a schedule change at all — many growers run 18/6 or even 20/4 for the entire life cycle, since autos flower based on age rather than light cycle.

Distance and intensity

Always measure light distance from the top of your canopy (the highest leaves) to the LED lens, not from the pot or the floor. Start new seedlings further away and at lower power, then gradually move the light closer and increase intensity as the plant shows healthy, vigorous growth. Going too strong too early is one of the most common causes of light stress, which shows up as bleached or yellowing patches on the topmost leaves.

Diagnosing light stress: before assuming it's the light, rule out overwatering, cold stress, and nutrient issues first — they can look similar. If it is light stress, raise the light immediately. Already-bleached tissue won't recover, but check new growth over the following days to confirm the fix worked.
