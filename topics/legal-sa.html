SA Legal Landscape

South Africa's cannabis laws have shifted significantly in recent years. Here's a general overview — always verify current details for your specific situation.

The 2018 Constitutional Court ruling

In September 2018, South Africa's Constitutional Court ruled that the private use and cultivation of cannabis by adults, in private spaces, for personal consumption, is protected under the constitutional right to privacy. This decriminalised personal private use and cultivation, though it did not establish a fully regulated commercial framework at the time.

The Cannabis for Private Purposes Act

Following the Constitutional Court ruling, South Africa passed the Cannabis for Private Purposes Act, which formalised rules around private adult use and cultivation, including provisions around the quantity an adult may possess and cultivate for personal use in a private space. The specific legal quantities and details have been refined through this legislation and related regulations.

What this generally means in practice

  • Adults may grow cannabis for personal use in a private space, such as their own home.
  • Public use, and use in view of or accessible to children, remains restricted.
  • Selling or distributing cannabis outside of the licensed commercial framework remains illegal.
  • Commercial cultivation and sale require separate licensing through SAHPRA (South African Health Products Regulatory Authority) and related regulatory bodies.
This is general information, not legal advice. Cannabis law is an evolving area, and specific details — quantity limits, commercial licensing requirements, and enforcement — can change. If your situation involves anything beyond small-scale private personal cultivation, especially commercial activity, consult current official sources or a legal professional familiar with South African cannabis law before proceeding.

Commercial cultivation

Operating a cannabis business — including cultivation for sale — falls under a separate, more complex regulatory framework requiring licensing through SAHPRA. This is a substantially different legal process from private personal cultivation, with its own compliance, quality, and reporting requirements. Anyone pursuing commercial cultivation should engage directly with current SAHPRA guidance and likely professional legal and regulatory support, since this framework continues to develop.
