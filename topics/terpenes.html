Terpenes & Cannabinoids

The compounds that give cannabis its character aren't just for show — they're chemically active and shape the entire experience.

What are cannabinoids

Cannabinoids are chemical compounds produced by the cannabis plant that interact with the body's endocannabinoid system. The two most well-known are THC (tetrahydrocannabinol), which is psychoactive, and CBD (cannabidiol), which is not psychoactive in the same way but is studied for various other effects. Cannabis plants produce dozens of other cannabinoids in smaller amounts, many of which are still being researched.

What are terpenes

Terpenes are aromatic compounds responsible for the distinct smell and flavour of different cannabis strains — the citrus, pine, diesel, or earthy notes you notice are almost entirely terpene-driven. They're produced in the same trichomes that produce cannabinoids, which is part of why proper harvest timing, drying, and curing (which preserve trichome integrity) directly affect how aromatic and flavourful the final product is.

Common terpenes and their associated profiles

  • Myrcene: Earthy, musky, sometimes described as having a slight clove note. One of the most common terpenes in cannabis overall.
  • Limonene: Bright citrus aroma, found in high concentrations in many fruity-smelling strains.
  • Pinene: Sharp pine scent, also found in rosemary and pine needles.
  • Caryophyllene: Peppery, spicy aroma — notably the only terpene known to also interact directly with cannabinoid receptors, blurring the line between terpene and cannabinoid effects.
  • Linalool: Floral, slightly lavender-like aroma.

Why curing matters for terpenes

Terpenes are volatile compounds, meaning they evaporate relatively easily, especially under heat or rapid drying. This is the core reason a slow, careful dry and an extended cure preserve aroma and flavour far better than rushing the process — fast, hot drying drives off a significant portion of the terpene content before you ever get to enjoy it.

The entourage effect: many researchers and growers believe cannabinoids and terpenes work together synergistically rather than independently — meaning the full spectrum of compounds in a strain may produce a different, often more nuanced effect than an isolated cannabinoid alone. This is part of why whole-plant cannabis is often described differently from isolated THC or CBD products.
